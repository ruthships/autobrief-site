PODCAST #10

June 10, 2026

This week's theme is dependency. The most powerful AI is increasingly something companies rent rather than build — and the real contest is over who owns the models, the chips, and the tools underneath. Anthropic shipped Claude Fable 5, its most capable public model yet, with fresh pricing and a data-retention catch enterprises need to read closely. Google agreed to pay SpaceX nearly a billion dollars a month to rent xAI's GPUs, turning raw compute into the business. Washington moved on two fronts to speed adoption and centralize the rules. And a Microsoft breach plus a major OpenCV release were reminders that AI's risks and gains often live in the plumbing. AI has become infrastructure — owned, rented, regulated, and fought over.

Anthropic Releases Claude Fable 5

Anthropic released Claude Fable 5, its most powerful publicly available model, in a new top tier above its previous flagship — priced at $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output, with a 30-day data-retention change enterprises should note.

Google Rents xAI's GPUs Through SpaceX

Google agreed to pay SpaceX roughly $920 million a month to rent about 110,000 Nvidia chips in xAI's data centers — around $30 billion over the term — as compute becomes the scarce, monetizable layer of the AI stack.

Google Ships Gemini Everywhere

Google made Gemini 3.5 Flash the default across its app and AI Mode in Search worldwide, and launched Gemini Spark, a round-the-clock personal agent wired into Workspace email and documents.

Nvidia's Bet on Physical AI

At its Taipei conference, Nvidia released Cosmos 3, an open model built for robots and self-driving cars to act in the real world, and confirmed its next-gen Vera Rubin platform is ramping to full production.

The Reckoning Beneath the Spending

New studies show returns lagging the hype — Bain found most firms missing AI savings targets, only 12% of CEOs report both lower costs and higher revenue, and US tech cut 142,000+ jobs even as budgets and capex climb.
